NVC & P.E.T. Parenting Communication Framework
1. Core Philosophy

Behavior is Communication: Every action a child or teenager takes, especially a disruptive one, is a tragic expression of an unmet need.

Connection Before Correction: You cannot effectively discipline or guide a child who feels misunderstood. Empathy and active listening must occur before any problem-solving or requests.

2. The 4 Steps of Nonviolent Communication (NVC)

Step 1: Observation: State exactly what you see or hear without adding judgment, evaluation, or exaggeration. (e.g., "I see three dirty plates on your desk," instead of "You are a slob.")

Step 2: Feeling: Identify the emotion you are experiencing. Avoid "faux feelings" that blame others. (e.g., "I feel overwhelmed and worried," instead of "I feel ignored by you.")

Step 3: Need: Connect the feeling to your universal human need. (e.g., "I need cooperation and order in the shared spaces of our home.")

Step 4: Request: Ask for a specific, actionable, and positive behavior. A request allows for a "no." If a "no" triggers punishment, it was a demand, not a request. (e.g., "Would you be willing to bring the plates to the sink before dinner?")

3. P.E.T. Active Listening (Parent Effectiveness Training)

The Goal: To decode the child's underlying message and reflect it back to them so they feel completely understood.

The Rule: Do not advise, fix, judge, or interrogate. Simply listen and mirror.

The Formula: "You are feeling [Emotion] because you need [Unmet Need]." (e.g., "You are feeling furious right now because you need autonomy over your own schedule.")

4. Common Unmet Needs in Children and Teenagers

Autonomy / Choice: The need to have control over their own bodies, space, and time.

Competence: The need to feel capable and trusted to do things right.

Connection / Belonging: The need to feel unconditionally loved and accepted, even when making mistakes.

Predictability / Safety: The need to know what to expect (often triggered during transitions between activities).

Physical Well-being: The need for sleep, food, movement, or rest.

5. The De-escalation Protocol

When a child is heightened (yelling, slamming doors), their logical brain is offline.

The parent must regulate their own nervous system first.

Use Active Listening to guess the child's feeling and need.

Wait until the child physically relaxes before introducing an NVC "I-Message" (Observation, Feeling, Need, Request).
